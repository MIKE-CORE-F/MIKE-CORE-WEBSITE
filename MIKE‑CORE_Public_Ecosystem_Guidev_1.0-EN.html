The crisis of modern IT
Page 3

The IT sector is in a structural crisis that is rarely named explicitly. Not because technology is failing, but because our way of organising no longer matches reality.

1. Complexity grows faster than structure

Modern systems consist of:

  • microservices and cloud platforms
  • API chains and distributed workloads
  • hybrid infrastructures and legacy systems

But the structures that are supposed to carry these systems are often:

  • ad‑hoc and historically grown
  • team‑based instead of ecosystemic
  • dependent on individual memory
  • not reproducible and hard to audit

2. Dependencies are the real bottleneck

It is not the technology that fails, but the invisible dependencies between teams, systems and processes. Frameworks such as Scrum or Kanban:

  • visualise work
  • organise rhythm
  • create ceremonies

But they do not manage dependencies. And dependencies are exactly where systems break.

3. Drift is everywhere

Drift is the spontaneous deviation from:

  • agreements and processes
  • structure and architecture
  • direction and quality

Drift arises automatically in any system that lacks ecosystemic stability. Frameworks do not detect drift. Ecosystems do.

4. Framework inflation

Organisations stack frameworks:

  • Scrum, SAFe, Kanban
  • ITIL, DevOps, Lean

But frameworks do not solve structural problems. They mask them with rhythm, ceremonies and process optimisation. The crisis is not technological, but architectural.

Why ecosystemic thinking is needed
Page 4

For years, the IT world has tried to control complexity with frameworks, methodologies and process optimisation. But frameworks are designed for teams, not for ecosystems. They focus on rhythm, ceremonies and work visualisation — not on stability, dependencies or reproducibility.

1. Frameworks optimise locally, ecosystems optimise globally

Scrum optimises one team. Kanban optimises flow. DevOps optimises collaboration. But no framework optimises:

  • the cohesion between teams
  • the stability of the whole
  • the dependencies between systems
  • the reproducibility of architecture

Ecosystems do.

2. Modern IT is too complex for team‑level solutions

An organisation does not consist of one team, but of:

  • dozens of teams
  • hundreds of systems
  • thousands of dependencies

Frameworks cannot carry this complexity. Ecosystems are built for it.

3. Ecosystems prevent chaos where frameworks do not see it

Frameworks detect:

  • delays and blockers
  • inefficiency and process issues

Ecosystems detect:

  • drift
  • dependency breaks
  • incoherence
  • architectural instability

That is the difference between symptoms and causes.

4. Ecosystems create calm instead of rhythm

Frameworks create rhythm. Ecosystems create calm. Calm is:

  • predictability
  • stability
  • reproducibility
  • coherence

Calm is the basis on which rhythm can exist — not the other way around.

What is an IT ecosystem?
Page 5

An IT ecosystem is not a framework, not a process model and not a methodology. It is an architectural layer that sits above frameworks and safeguards the stability of the whole.

An ecosystem consists of:

1. Principles

The philosophical basis that determines how the system behaves.

2. Axes

Fundamental perspectives that structure direction, identity and transition.
(The internal workings of the axes remain closed in this public edition.)

3. Domains

Large functional areas, each carrying a part of the ecosystem.
(The exact domain structure remains internal.)

4. Modules

Loose, portable components that operate within the ecosystem.

5. Validation chains

Mechanisms that detect drift, inconsistency and dependency breaks.
(The full chains remain confidential.)

6. Governance

Not bureaucratic, but ecosystemic: minimal rules, maximum stability.

Framework vs. ecosystem

Frameworks:

  • focus on teams
  • organise rhythm
  • define ceremonies
  • are minimalist and context‑dependent

Ecosystems:

  • focus on the whole
  • create stability
  • manage dependencies
  • prevent drift and are reproducible

Frameworks are tools. Ecosystems are foundations.

The philosophy of MIKE‑CORE
Page 6

MIKE‑CORE is built on three core principles that together form the basis of ecosystemic IT architecture:

1. Stability over rhythm

Frameworks focus on rhythm: sprints, ceremonies, cadence. But rhythm without stability is organised chaos. MIKE‑CORE creates stability through:

  • reproducible structures
  • coherent domains
  • drift control
  • dependency management

Stability is not a luxury — it is a prerequisite.

2. Coherence over speed

Speed without coherence leads to:

  • technical debt
  • inconsistency
  • architectural breaks
  • unpredictable risks

Coherence means that everything fits together logically, every module strengthens the whole and every decision is ecosystemically responsible. Coherence is the oxygen of a healthy IT landscape.

3. Reproducibility over improvisation

Improvisation is the enemy of stability. Reproducibility is the basis of:

  • audit safety
  • scalability
  • reliability
  • predictability

An ecosystem that is not reproducible is not an ecosystem — it is a collection of coincidences.

High‑level architecture (public version)
Page 7

The full architecture of MIKE‑CORE is deliberately kept closed in this public edition. What is described below is the conceptual top layer — enough to understand the ecosystem, but without exposing the internal blueprint, validation chains or domain logic.

The high‑level architecture consists of four visible components:

1. The axes (I, R, T axis)

The axes form the three fundamental perspectives of the ecosystem. They structure:

  • Identity (I axis)
  • Direction (R axis)
  • Transition (T axis)

Every decision, module or domain is filtered through these axes. They ensure that the ecosystem remains coherent, reproducible and stable.
(The internal workings, mapping and validation chains of the axes remain confidential.)

2. Domains (conceptual layer)

An ecosystem consists of different domains, each representing a part of IT reality. In the public layer we only describe:

  • that domains exist
  • that together they form a coherent whole
  • that they are ecosystemically connected

The exact domain structure, interrelations and validation chains remain internal.

3. Modules

Modules are portable ecosystemic components that:

  • fulfil a specific function
  • can move independently
  • are reproducible
  • are drift‑safe
  • remain coherent within the whole

Examples of public modules include:

  • Framework Diagnostic Modules (such as a Scrum Diagnostic Module)
  • Stability Check Modules
  • Ecosystemic Flow Modules

4. Ecosystemic governance

Governance in MIKE‑CORE is not bureaucracy. It is a lightweight but indestructible structure that:

  • safeguards stability
  • manages dependencies
  • detects drift
  • enforces coherence

The ecosystem operates with minimal rules, but maximum stability.

Ecosystemic principles
Page 8

MIKE‑CORE is built on a set of principles that determine how the ecosystem behaves. These principles are not optional — they form the ecosystemic laws of nature.

1. Identity (I axis)

Identity determines:

  • what a module is
  • what a domain represents
  • which role a component plays
  • which boundaries it has

Without identity, confusion, overlap and drift arise.

2. Direction (R axis)

Direction determines:

  • where the ecosystem is heading
  • which goals have priority
  • how decisions are made
  • how coherence is safeguarded

Direction prevents teams or systems from disappearing into their own micro‑reality.

3. Transition (T axis)

Transition determines:

  • how change happens
  • how stability is preserved during evolution
  • how dependencies are protected
  • how drift is prevented during movement

Transition is the art of changing without breaking.

4. Drift control

Drift is the greatest enemy of any IT landscape. It arises spontaneously when:

  • teams improvise
  • documentation is missing
  • architecture is not monitored
  • dependencies shift
  • processes become inconsistent

MIKE‑CORE contains ecosystemic mechanisms to:

  • detect drift
  • isolate drift
  • correct drift

An ecosystem without drift control is a house of cards.

MIKE‑CORE guarantees reproducibility through:

  • domains that enforce coherence

When a system is reproducible:

  • declining risks become visible
  • audits become simple
  • dependencies become manageable
  • innovation becomes safer

Reproducibility is not a brake, but an accelerator.

Coherence means:

2. Stability: the system stays upright, even under pressure

Stability means:

  • reproducible processes
  • predictable results
  • consistent architecture
  • minimal drift
  • controlled dependencies

A stable ecosystem can:

  • grow without breaking
  • change without chaos
  • allow teams to change without loss of knowledge
  • withstand audits without stress

3. Why frameworks cannot guarantee stability

Frameworks:

  • are team‑based
  • are rhythm‑based
  • are context‑dependent
  • lack architectural depth

They can simulate stability, but not guarantee it. Ecosystems can.
